Introduction

Every child is born with the right to learn, a right that should never depend on where they live, their gender, or how much their family earns.
Education is the path that provides opportunities, fights poverty, and empowers future generations. Yet today, millions of children cannot access this right. Over 251 million school-age youths remain out of the classroom, since their opportunity to learn is limited by barriers that they did not choose.

This report analyses the global education crisis. By examining how socioeconomic status, gender, and geography affect school attendance, it reveals the deep inequalities that still shape the future of millions of youths. We aim to boost awareness and drive action, because every child deserves the chance to learn, grow, and thrive.

Where children are missing out on school

Out of schools: regions with the highest number of children not in education.

Across the globe, the chance to go to school is still defined by geography. In Sub-Saharan Africa, almost 794 million children remain out of school. A shocking number that reflects issues rooted in access and opportunity. In Central and Southern Asia and Eastern and South-Eastern Asia, the story is similar, with each region counting over 500 million children and youths excluded from education. In opposition, regions like Europe, North America, and Oceania score lower numbers, meaning that most youths are enrolled in education. The contrast is clear. A child‚Äôs right to learn still depends heavily on where they are born, reminding us that inequality in education is still one of the world‚Äôs most severe issue.

How learning shapes a longer life

Higher school attendance is linked to longer, healthier lives.

In places where fewer children attend school, people often live shorter lives. Countries with attendance rates under 50% also tend to have some of the lowest life expectancies, sometimes even under 50 years. These are environments where children have limited access not only to education but also to healthcare, safety, and support. On the other hand, countries where school attendance is high see people living into their 60s or 70s. These nations typically have stronger education systems, better healthcare, and an overall more stable environment.

The silent gap between boys and girls

School attendance still shows clear gender disparities across countries.

In many countries, girls are still less likely to attend school than boys, highlighting a still existing gender gap in education.
The widest gap is found in Afghanistan, where boys exceed girls in classrooms by almost 20 percentage points. Similar situations appear in Yemen, Guinea, and Eritrea, where girls continue to be excluded from education. Even in countries like the Turks and Caicos Islands and Saint Lucia, where the overall attendance rate is high, boys still represent the majority of students.

A decade of progress: ups and downs

Global school attendance shifted greatly from 2010 to 2020, rising sharply, facing drops, and lastly reaching its highest point.

  • Between 2010 and 2020, global school attendance went through both progress and difficulty. It started at just 34%, showing how many children could not still access their right to learn.
  • By 2012, attendance had risen to 77%, showing big improvements in education access.
  • But in 2013, it fell to 54% and remained inconsistent during the decade.
  • From 2016 on, there was a slow and consistent increase, reaching 88% in 2020, the highest level scored in the considered period. This growth may represent stronger efforts, better policies, and initiatives affecting more children all around the world.

The overall trend is positive, but its inconsistent shifting reminds us that progress is not granted. Investment and global collaboration are essential to make sure that every youth has the chance to stay in school.

How the world went back to school

In 2019, the trend became stronger, with most countries scoring between 85% and 95%, and only a few still below 60%. Surprisingly, despite the global pandemic of 2020, attendance reached its highest levels, reflecting well functioning policy efforts. Overall, the decade ends with progress.

Conclusion

This report highlights that access to education remains unequal, with attendance rates still linked to income, location, and gender. While some countries have reached 90% attendance, others, remain below 25%.
Sub-Saharan Africa scores the largest amount of out-of-school youths, and in many countries, girls continue to stay behind.
Between 2010 and 2020, global attendance increased from 34% to 88%, showing that progress is possible but also inconsistent.
To close the gap, we must invest in low-income countries, prioritise girls‚Äô education, and support the right to education in areas affected by conflicts. Schools in rural areas need better infrastructure, teachers need support, and communities must be engaged.
